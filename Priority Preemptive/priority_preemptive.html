Priority Preemptive ALGORITHM


Priority Preemptive Scheduling algorithm is a CPU scheduling method that assigns priorities to each process based on certain criteria, such as the importance or urgency of the process. The process with the highest priority is given the CPU for execution. In preemptive priority scheduling, if a new process with a higher priority arrives in the ready queue while a lower priority process is currently running, the CPU is preempted, and the higher priority process is executed. This ensures that critical tasks are executed promptly, even if lower priority tasks are already running. Priority scheduling can be either static, where priorities are assigned at the start and remain fixed throughout the process's lifetime, or dynamic, where priorities may change during execution based on certain criteria. Dynamic priority scheduling algorithms may use aging mechanisms to prevent starvation of lower priority processes. Priority Preemptive Scheduling is commonly used in real-time systems, where tasks must meet strict deadlines and higher priority tasks must be completed before lower priority ones. However, it can suffer from the problem of priority inversion, where a lower priority task holds a resource needed by a higher priority task, causing the higher priority task to wait unnecessarily. To mitigate this, priority inheritance or priority ceiling protocols can be implemented.
